From N-grams to Neural Language Models

How language modeling started, why it worked, and where it broke down

The problem: predict the next token

Every language model — from the simplest statistical model of the 1980s to GPT-4 — is solving the same problem:

Given a sequence of tokens so far, what token is most likely to come next?

A token is just a unit of text. It might be a character, a word, or a subword chunk like transform + er. The details don't matter for this lesson — what matters is that the model reads left to right and at each step emits a probability distribution over all possible next tokens.

"Language model" is the formal name for a system that assigns probabilities to sequences of text. A good language model gives high probability to grammatical, sensible text and low probability to gibberish.

N-grams: counting your way to language

The earliest approach was almost embarrassingly simple: count how often each sequence of words appears in a large corpus, then use those counts as your probability estimates.

A unigram model ignores all context. It just tracks how often each word appears overall:

P("the") = 62,000 / 1,000,000 = 6.2% P("cat") = 1,200 / 1,000,000 = 0.12% P("zzxjy") = 0 / 1,000,000 = 0%

A bigram model uses the previous word as context: given the word "the", how likely is each word to follow?

P("cat" | "the") = count("the cat") / count("the") = 0.8% P("dog" | "the") = count("the dog") / count("the") = 0.6% P("bank" | "the") = count("the bank") / count("the") = 0.4%

A trigram model uses the previous two words. An n-gram model uses the previous n−1 words. More context → better predictions.

This is a lookup table, not learning. No neural network. No training. You just count occurrences in your corpus and normalize.

Why n-grams worked (surprisingly well)

For decades, n-gram models were the state of the art in speech recognition and machine translation. They were fast, interpretable, and easy to build. Google's early search autocomplete ran on n-gram statistics over billions of web pages.

The intuition holds: most language is locally predictable. "New York" almost always follows "New" in that order. "I am going to" is usually followed by a verb. You can get a lot of mileage just from local co-occurrence statistics.

Where n-grams break down: the sparsity wall

The fundamental problem with n-grams is combinatorial explosion.

English has roughly 170,000 words. A bigram table has 170,000 × 170,000 = 29 billion possible entries. A trigram table: 4.9 trillion. Most of those entries will never appear in any corpus — no matter how large.

This is the sparsity problem: the model has seen "the cat sat" but never "the cat perched" — so it assigns zero probability to that perfectly valid phrase. Real corpora can never cover the full space of possible n-grams.

Researchers invented workarounds — smoothing, back-off, interpolation — to redistribute some probability mass to unseen n-grams. These helped, but they were patches over a deeper flaw.

The deeper flaw: n-gram models have no concept of meaning. They know that "dog" follows "the" at a certain rate, but they don't know that "dog" and "cat" are both animals, or that "perched" and "sat" both describe a resting posture. Each word is just an opaque symbol.

An n-gram model treats "dog" and "hot dog" and "dog-eared" as completely unrelated tokens. It has no way to generalize across words that mean similar things.

The neural turn: word embeddings and learned representations

In 2003, Yoshua Bengio and colleagues published "A Neural Probabilistic Language Model" — one of the papers that launched modern deep learning for NLP.

The key insight: instead of treating each word as an opaque symbol (a lookup table row), represent each word as a dense vector of numbers in a high-dimensional space. That vector is the word's embedding.

Then, instead of looking up counts, train a neural network to predict the next word — and in doing so, learn what those embedding vectors should be.

The result: words that appear in similar contexts end up with similar embedding vectors. The model learns, purely from text, that "dog" and "cat" are similar. That "Paris" and "London" are both cities. That "walked" and "ran" are both past-tense verbs.

← semantic space → "king" [0.92, 0.14, 0.73, ...] "queen" [0.89, 0.19, 0.71, ...] ← close to "king" "dog" [0.12, 0.88, 0.04, ...] "cat" [0.11, 0.91, 0.06, ...] ← close to "dog" "Paris" [0.55, 0.22, 0.91, ...] "London" [0.52, 0.25, 0.89, ...] ← close to "Paris"

This solved the sparsity problem at a conceptual level: you no longer need to have seen "the cat perched" to predict it's plausible, because you've learned that "cat" ≈ "dog" and you have seen "the dog perched". The representation generalizes.

Word2Vec (2013) and GloVe (2014) turned this into standalone tools that could be used as inputs to any NLP system. The idea of representing meaning as a point in a vector space became foundational.

The remaining problem: fixed context windows

Early neural language models still had a hard limit on how much context they could use — typically just a handful of previous words, encoded as a fixed-size window. You'd concatenate the embeddings of the last 5 words and feed that to the network.

This is better than counting, but it's still a lookup into a small fixed window. The model can't connect "he" in sentence 12 to the name it refers to in sentence 1.

To handle longer sequences — paragraphs, documents, whole conversations — you needed a way to process sequences of arbitrary length and carry information forward through them.

That's what recurrent neural networks attempted to do next. That's the subject of Lesson 2.

The arc so far: N-grams counted local co-occurrence but couldn't generalize across meaning. Neural language models introduced word embeddings that encode meaning as vectors and generalize to unseen combinations — but still struggled with long-range context. The next step was handling sequences.
